First 90 Days as a Property Manager Introduction

How to Use This Playbook

Congratulations on your new role as a Property Manager. Whether you're stepping into your first PM position or taking over a new property, the next 90 days are critical. They'll set the tone for your tenure and establish your reputation with residents, staff, and leadership.

This playbook breaks down your first three months into manageable, week-by-week chunks. Follow it sequentially, or jump to the section most relevant to where you are today.

What This Playbook Covers

  • Week 1-2: Orientation ‚Äî Survive your first days, meet key people, get system access, and understand what you're walking into.
  • Week 3-4: Learn the Property ‚Äî Deep-dive into the rent roll, understand the physical asset, and identify the landmines.
  • Week 5-6: Build Relationships ‚Äî Connect with residents, align with your team, and set expectations with your supervisor.
  • Week 7-8: Take Ownership ‚Äî Run your first meetings, establish your rhythm, and start making the property yours.
  • Week 9-10: Understand the Numbers ‚Äî Master the budget, learn what drives NOI, and speak the language of ownership.
  • Week 11-12: Drive Results ‚Äî Execute on occupancy, collections, renewals, and prepare for your 90-day review.

Who This Playbook Is For

  • Experienced PMs taking over a new property and needing a systematic approach

  • Career changers entering property management from hospitality, retail, or other customer-facing roles

Orientation

Hit the Ground Running

Your first two weeks are about survival and setup. You're drinking from a firehose ‚Äî that's normal. Focus on getting access, meeting people, and understanding what you've inherited. Don't try to change anything yet.

Day 1 Survival Checklist

‚úì Complete Before You Leave Day 1

  • Get keys to the office, amenities, and model units
  • Obtain property management software login (Yardi, RealPage, AppFolio, etc.)
  • Set up your email and phone system
  • Locate emergency contact list and after-hours procedures
  • Review the property's emergency action plan
  • Introduce yourself to whoever is on-site today
  • Walk the property ‚Äî just observe, don't judge yet
  • Identify where key documents are stored (leases, invoices, vendor files)

Key People to Meet

Your success depends on relationships. In the first two weeks, schedule time with:

Person Why They Matter What to Ask
Maintenance Supervisor Controls resident satisfaction and curb appeal What's the biggest maintenance challenge right now?
Lead Leasing Agent Drives revenue and knows prospect objections Why do people choose us? Why do they say no?
Regional Director Your boss ‚Äî sets expectations and provides support What does success look like in 90 days?
Previous PM (if available) Knows the history and hidden problems What do you wish you'd known earlier?
Top 3 Vendors Landscaping, cleaning, HVAC ‚Äî your operational backbone Are we current on payments? Any open issues?

Watch Out: The "Welcoming Committee" Some staff will try to influence you early ‚Äî complaining about coworkers, policies, or the previous PM. Listen politely, but don't commit to anything. Form your own opinions based on observation.

Systems Access Checklist

You can't manage what you can't access. Push hard to get these logins in your first week:

  • Company email and calendar

  • Online reputation sites (Google Business, Yelp admin)
  • Company intranet / shared drives

Property Walk Checklist

Walk the entire property in your first week with a critical eye. You're not looking to fix things yet ‚Äî you're cataloging.

‚úì Exterior Inspection

  • Curb appeal: signage, landscaping, parking lot condition
  • Building exteriors: paint, siding, gutters, lighting
  • Amenities: pool, gym, clubhouse, dog park condition
  • Common areas: hallways, stairwells, laundry rooms
  • Dumpster areas: cleanliness, capacity, enclosures
  • Safety: lighting, trip hazards, railings, fire equipment

‚úì Model/Vacant Units

  • Model unit presentation and cleanliness
  • Vacant unit count and make-ready status
  • Down units: why are they down? What's the plan?
  • Unit condition overall: flooring, appliances, fixtures

Questions to Ask the Previous PM

If you can talk to your predecessor, these questions will save you months of learning:

  1. Who are the problem residents I should know about?
  2. Which vendors are great? Which ones should I replace?
  3. What projects are in progress or promised to residents?
  4. Where are the bodies buried? (What problems haven't surfaced yet?)
  5. What would you do differently if you were starting over?
  6. Who on the team is a star? Who needs coaching?
  7. What does the Regional Director really care about?

First 90 Days as a Property Manager Week 3-4
3-4

Learn the Property

Inside and Out

Now that you've survived orientation, it's time to truly understand your property. This means getting intimate with the numbers, the physical asset, and the people who live there. By the end of Week 4, you should be able to answer any question about your property without looking it up.

Unit Mix & Floorplan Mastery

Memorize your unit mix. When leadership asks, "How many 2-bedrooms do you have?" ‚Äî you answer instantly.

‚úì Know These Numbers Cold

  • Total unit count
  • Unit mix by floorplan (Studios, 1BR, 2BR, 3BR)
  • Square footage ranges
  • Rent ranges by floorplan
  • Which buildings have which floorplans
  • Premium units (upgraded, views, patios)
  • ADA-accessible unit locations

Rent Roll Deep-Dive

The rent roll tells the story of your property. Spend serious time with it.

What to Look For:

Metric What It Tells You Red Flag If...
Rent vs. Market Are you leaving money on the table? >10% below market on renewals
Lease Expirations Workload and renewal opportunities >20% expiring in one month
Move-In Dates Resident tenure and stability High turnover (avg tenure <12 months)
Concessions How hard you're working for leases Heavy concessions + low occupancy
Balance Owed Collection problems >5% of residents with balance

Lease Expiration Calendar

Map out the next 12 months of lease expirations. This drives your renewal campaign timing and helps you anticipate vacancy.

üí°
The 120-Day Rule Start renewal conversations 120 days before lease expiration. This gives residents time to decide and gives you time to market the unit if they decline. Waiting until 60 days is too late.
First 90 Days as a Property Manager Week 3-4

Delinquency Assessment

Understand who owes money and why. Delinquency is often inherited ‚Äî don't feel guilty about it, but do own fixing it.

‚úì Delinquency Audit Steps

  • Pull a full aging report (current, 1-30, 31-60, 61-90, 90+)
  • Calculate total delinquency as a % of potential rent
  • Identify your top 10 delinquent accounts by dollar amount
  • Review each file: payment history, communication log, legal status
  • Note which accounts have payment plans in place
  • Identify accounts that should be in eviction but aren't

Maintenance Backlog Review

Open work orders are promises to residents. Understand what's been promised and what's overdue.

Priority SLA Target Your Property's Avg
Emergency (no heat, flood, lock-out) 4 hours ______
Urgent (no hot water, AC out, appliance down) 24 hours ______
Routine (minor repairs, cosmetic) 72 hours ______
Scheduled (not time-sensitive) 7 days ______

The Maintenance Reputation Nothing kills resident satisfaction faster than ignored work orders. If you inherit a backlog, communicate with affected residents immediately. Acknowledgment and a timeline beats silence.

Vendor Roster & Contract Status

You rely on vendors for landscaping, cleaning, pest control, HVAC, and more. Know who they are and what you're paying.

‚úì Vendor Review

  • List all active vendors and their services
  • Verify contract terms and expiration dates
  • Check payment status ‚Äî are we current?
  • Review insurance certificates (valid and adequate?)
  • Ask staff: Which vendors are great? Which are problems?
  • Get vendor contact info in your phone
‚úÖ
Quick Win: The Vendor Reset Call your top 5 vendors personally. Introduce yourself, confirm the scope of work, and ask if there are any outstanding issues. This shows you're engaged and often surfaces problems before they escalate.
First 90 Days as a Property Manager Week 5-6
5-6

Build Relationships

That Matter

You've learned the systems and the property. Now it's time to invest in people. The relationships you build in these two weeks will determine how much support you get when things go sideways ‚Äî and they will.

Resident Meet-and-Greet Strategies

Residents want to know who's running the show. Make yourself visible and approachable.

Tactics That Work:

  • Office Hours: Set specific times when you're available for walk-ins. Post them on your door and in common areas.
  • Property Walks: Walk the property at different times ‚Äî morning, evening, weekends. Stop and chat when you see residents.
  • Welcome Calls: Personally call residents who moved in recently. Ask how move-in went and if they need anything.
  • Community Events: If budget allows, host a simple meet-and-greet. Coffee and donuts on a Saturday morning works.
  • Problem Resolution: When you solve a problem, follow up personally. "I wanted to make sure that work order was handled to your satisfaction."
üí°
The Name Game Learn residents' names. Greet them by name when you see them. This small act of recognition builds loyalty faster than any amenity upgrade.

Maintenance Tech 1-on-1s

Your maintenance team makes or breaks resident satisfaction. Invest time in understanding their world.

Questions for Each Tech:

  1. What do you like most about working here?
  2. What's your biggest frustration right now?
  3. Do you have the tools and supplies you need?
  4. What building or unit type gives you the most problems?
  5. If you could change one thing about how we operate, what would it be?
  6. How do you prefer to receive work orders and communicate?
‚ö†Ô∏è
Maintenance Respect Never treat maintenance as "less than" the office staff. Skilled techs are hard to find. Losing a good one costs you thousands in turnover and resident complaints.
First 90 Days as a Property Manager Week 5-6

Leasing Team Alignment

Your leasing team is the revenue engine. Make sure you're aligned on goals, process, and expectations.

‚úì Leasing Alignment Meeting Agenda

  • Review current occupancy and leasing goal
  • Discuss traffic sources: where are leads coming from?
  • Walk through the tour path together ‚Äî is it compelling?
  • Review follow-up process: how quickly? How many touches?
  • Discuss common objections and how we overcome them
  • Clarify approval authority: what can they approve without you?
  • Set expectations for communication and reporting

Regional Director Expectations

Your RD is your most important relationship. Get crystal clear on what they expect.

Questions to Ask Your RD:

  1. What does success look like at 90 days? At 6 months? At 1 year?
  2. What are the non-negotiables I need to know about?
  3. How do you prefer to communicate? How often?
  4. What authority do I have? What decisions need your approval?
  5. What happened with the previous PM? What should I do differently?
  6. What's the biggest risk at this property right now?
  7. How will you evaluate my performance?
‚úÖ
The Weekly Update Start sending a brief weekly update to your RD. Include: occupancy, leasing activity, delinquency, any issues, and your priorities for next week. This builds trust and keeps surprises to a minimum.

First 90 Days as a Property Manager Week 7-8
7-8

Take Ownership

Make It Yours

You've observed, learned, and built relationships. Now it's time to lead. By the end of Week 8, your team should feel your presence and direction. You're no longer the new person ‚Äî you're the PM.

Running Your First Team Meeting

A well-run team meeting sets the tone for your leadership. Here's a simple agenda that works:

‚úì Weekly Team Meeting Agenda (30-45 min)

  • Wins (5 min): What went well this week? Celebrate something.
  • Numbers (5 min): Occupancy, traffic, leases, delinquency, work orders
  • Focus (10 min): What's the #1 priority this week?
  • Issues (10 min): What's blocking us? Who needs help?
  • Announcements (5 min): Corporate updates, policy changes, events
  • Close (2 min): Recap action items, thank the team
üí°
Meeting Rules Start on time. End on time. No phones. Everyone speaks. Meetings without action items are just conversations.

Establishing Your Daily Rhythm

Consistency builds trust. When your team knows what to expect, they perform better.

Sample Daily Rhythm:

  • Morning (first 30 min): Check overnight emails, review today's move-ins/outs, scan work order queue
  • Mid-morning: Property walk ‚Äî different route each day
  • Before lunch: Touch base with maintenance supervisor
  • Afternoon: Focused work block ‚Äî renewals, collections calls, vendor management
  • End of day: Review tomorrow's calendar, respond to any urgent items, send updates if needed

Identifying Your Top 3 Problems

You can't fix everything at once. By Week 7, you should have a clear view of the property's biggest issues. Pick your top 3 and focus relentlessly.

Common Top 3 Problems:

  1. Occupancy: Below target, need to drive leasing
  2. Delinquency: Collections are behind, need to implement process
  3. Maintenance backlog: Work orders piling up, resident satisfaction suffering
  4. Team performance: Someone not pulling their weight
  5. Curb appeal: Property looks tired, affecting leasing
  6. Renewals: Too many residents leaving, need retention focus
‚ö†Ô∏è
Focus Beats Scattered Resist the urge to fix 10 things at once. Deep focus on 3 problems will yield better results than shallow attention to 10. You can always add more after you've made progress.

Reporting Cadence with Your RD

Don't wait for your RD to ask for updates. Proactive communication builds trust.

Suggested Reporting Rhythm:

  • Daily: Only if there's a crisis or major issue
  • Weekly: Brief email update (occupancy, issues, priorities)
  • Monthly: Formal report with financials, KPIs, and narrative
  • Quarterly: In-depth review meeting, goal-setting for next quarter

The "No Surprises" Rule Your RD should never learn about a problem from someone else first. If something bad happens ‚Äî or is about to happen ‚Äî tell them immediately. Bad news doesn't age well.

Keep a running log of your first 90 days. This will be invaluable for your review and your own professional development.

‚úì Track These Items

  • Problems you identified and how you addressed them
  • Quick wins you achieved

  • Skills you developed or need to develop
  • Feedback you received (positive and constructive)

9-10

Understand the Numbers

Master the Budget

You can't manage what you don't understand. Property management is ultimately a financial business, and your ability to read, interpret, and influence the numbers separates good PMs from great ones. These two weeks are about becoming fluent in the language of ownership.

Reading and Understanding the Budget

Every property operates on an annual budget. This document is your financial roadmap ‚Äî it tells you what's expected for revenue, expenses, and profitability.

Budget Components:

Section What It Contains Your Influence
Gross Potential Rent (GPR) Total rent if 100% occupied at market rates Indirect ‚Äî market sets rates
Loss to Lease Gap between GPR and actual rents charged High ‚Äî renewal negotiations
Vacancy Loss Revenue lost to empty units High ‚Äî occupancy management
Concessions Move-in specials, free rent High ‚Äî leasing strategy
Bad Debt Rent you won't collect High ‚Äî collections process
Other Income Fees, parking, laundry, pet rent Medium ‚Äî enforcement
Operating Expenses Payroll, utilities, R&M, contracts High ‚Äî cost control

Revenue vs. Expenses Breakdown

üí°
The Simple Formula NOI = Total Revenue - Operating Expenses

Net Operating Income (NOI) is the number ownership cares about most. Everything you do either increases revenue or decreases expenses ‚Äî both flow to NOI.

Revenue Levers You Control:

  • Occupancy rate (fewer vacant days)
  • Renewal rent increases (closing loss to lease)
  • Concession discipline (giving away less)
  • Collections (reducing bad debt)
  • Fee income (pet rent, parking, late fees)

Expense Levers You Control:

  • Vendor costs (negotiation, competitive bidding)
  • Utility management (common area efficiency)
  • Turn costs (reducing make-ready expenses)
  • Overtime management
  • Supply purchasing

NOI and What Drives It

Net Operating Income is how properties are valued. A property worth $10M at a 6% cap rate generates $600K NOI. Every dollar you add to NOI increases property value by ~$16-17 at that cap rate.

Variance Analysis Basics

Each month, you'll compare actual results to budget. This is called variance analysis. Learn to explain the "why" behind every significant variance.

Variance Analysis Questions:

  1. What's the variance? (Actual vs. Budget, $ and %)
  2. Is it favorable or unfavorable? (Did we beat budget or miss?)
  3. Is it timing or permanent? (Will it reverse, or are we off-track?)
  4. What caused it? (Root cause: market, operations, one-time event?)
  5. What's the action? (What are we doing about it?)
‚ö†Ô∏è
The 5% Rule Any line item that's more than 5% off budget (or $1,000+, whichever is less) deserves an explanation. Don't wait for your RD to ask ‚Äî include explanations in your monthly report.

CapEx vs. OpEx

Not all expenses are equal. Understanding the difference matters for budgeting and decision-making.

Operating Expenses (OpEx) Capital Expenses (CapEx)
Definition Day-to-day costs to run the property Major improvements that add value or extend life
Examples Repairs, supplies, utilities, payroll New roof, HVAC replacement, parking lot resurface
Budget Impact Hits NOI immediately Usually separate capital budget
Your Role Manage daily, stay within budget Identify needs, request funding

Questions to Ask About Budget Assumptions

‚úì Budget Review Checklist

  • What occupancy rate is assumed? Is it realistic?
  • What rent growth is built in? How does it compare to market?
  • What's budgeted for bad debt? Does it match recent history?
  • Are there any known capital needs not in the budget?
  • What contract renewals are coming that might change costs?
  • Is payroll realistic given current staffing?
  • What's the biggest risk to hitting this budget?

Drive Results

Execute and Deliver

The final two weeks of your first 90 days are about execution. You've learned, built relationships, and taken ownership. Now it's time to deliver measurable results and prepare for your review.

Occupancy Push Strategies

If occupancy is below target, these are your levers:

Short-Term Tactics (This Week):

  • Call every pending applicant ‚Äî what's holding them up?
  • Review every lead from the past 30 days ‚Äî any follow-up opportunities?
  • Audit your online listings ‚Äî photos current? Pricing competitive?
  • Offer a limited-time concession to close fence-sitters
  • Ask current residents for referrals (offer a bonus)

Medium-Term Tactics (This Month):

  • Refresh model unit staging
  • Improve curb appeal at entrance
  • Train leasing team on objection handling
  • Evaluate marketing spend ‚Äî is it working?
  • Consider broker outreach for corporate/relocation leads

Delinquency Reduction Plan

Collections require consistent process. Implement this if you don't have one:

Day of Month Action
1st Rent is due
2nd-4th Friendly reminder calls/texts to anyone unpaid
5th Late fee applied (per lease)
6th-10th Personal phone calls, payment arrangements discussed
10th Demand letter / Pay or Quit notice (per state law)
15th File for eviction if no payment or arrangement
üí°
Collections Mindset Be firm but fair. Most residents who fall behind want to pay ‚Äî they just need a plan. But don't let sympathy become a business problem. Unpaid rent hurts everyone, including residents who pay on time.
First 90 Days as a Property Manager Week 11-12

Renewal Campaign Basics

Renewals are cheaper than new leases. A strong renewal program protects occupancy and reduces turn costs.

Renewal Timeline:

  • 120 days out: Generate renewal offer, mail/email to resident
  • 90 days out: Personal follow-up call if no response
  • 60 days out: Final push ‚Äî meet in person if needed
  • 45 days out: If declining, begin marketing the unit
  • 30 days out: Confirm move-out date, schedule pre-move inspection

Renewal Conversation Tips:

  1. Lead with appreciation: "We value having you here..."
  2. Present the offer clearly: term, rent, any incentives
  3. Highlight what's improved since they moved in
  4. Ask about any concerns and address them
  5. Create urgency: "This offer is good until [date]"

Work Order SLA Management

Track and improve your maintenance response times:

‚úì Weekly Maintenance Review

  • Count of open work orders by priority
  • Average days to completion by category
  • Any work orders over 7 days old? Why?
  • Resident callbacks (work not done right first time)
  • Parts on order holding up completions?

Preparing for Your 90-Day Review

Don't wait for your RD to structure the conversation. Come prepared with:

‚úì 90-Day Review Preparation

  • Summary of property condition when you started
  • Key metrics: then vs. now (occupancy, delinquency, work orders)
  • Top 3 accomplishments with specific examples
  • Top 3 challenges and how you're addressing them
  • What you've learned about the property and team
  • Goals for the next 90 days
  • What support you need from your RD
  • Questions you have about your performance
‚úÖ
Own the Narrative Your 90-day review is a chance to tell your story. If you don't frame it, someone else will. Come with data, examples, and a clear view of the path forward.

You've Got This

The first 90 days are a marathon, not a sprint. Some days you'll feel overwhelmed. Some days you'll wonder if you made the right choice. That's normal.

What matters is showing up every day, learning constantly, and caring about your residents, your team, and your property. The tactical skills will come with time. The relationships you build and the reputation you establish in these first 90 days will carry you for years.

"Property management isn't about buildings. It's about people ‚Äî the residents who call your property home, the team members who depend on your leadership, and the owners who trust you with their investment. Take care of the people, and the numbers take care of themselves." ‚Äî Courtney Gordon

Key Takeaways

  2. Week 3-4: Learn everything ‚Äî rent roll, property condition, delinquency, vendors
  3. Week 5-6: Build relationships ‚Äî residents, team, RD, vendors
  4. Week 7-8: Take ownership ‚Äî run meetings, establish rhythm, pick your battles
  5. Week 9-10: Master the numbers ‚Äî budget, NOI, variance analysis
  6. Week 11-12: Drive results ‚Äî execute on occupancy, collections, renewals
